About

Benjamin Daßler

I am a post-doctoral researcher and principal investigator of the DFG-funded project The Politics of Contender Institutions (POLCON) at the Chair of Global Governance and Public Policy at the Geschwister-Scholl-Institute for Political Science, LMU Munich.

I am an international political economist with a particular interest in how global rules get made, contested, and rewritten. My work asks: who shapes the international institutions that govern trade, technology, and finance — and who benefits when those rules change? I focus on three interconnected areas: how states compete over international institutions when the global balance of power shifts; how Europe and other democracies respond to economic vulnerabilities by reducing strategic dependencies — a process known as de-risking — across sectors like digital infrastructure, AI, and critical raw materials; and how authoritarian-aligned actors exploit the openness of international organisations and technical standard-setting bodies to quietly embed rules that serve their interests.

I completed my doctorate summa cum laude at LMU Munich, hold an M.A. in Political Science from LMU Munich and a B.A. in Social Science with a minor in Political Economy from the University of Augsburg. Since April 2024, I lead the three-year DFG-funded project POLCON, which analyses the politics of contender institutions in regime complexes across development finance, digital governance, and climate and energy governance. → Visit the POLCON project page

In my research, I apply a broad spectrum of methods, from qualitative process tracing and network analysis to LLM-based text analysis and quantitative regression designs.

📖 Monograph · Oxford University Press · 2024
The Institutional Topology of International Regime Complexes:
Mapping Inter-Institutional Structures in Global Governance
Part of the Transformations in Governance series (OUP). The first book to systematically compare the network-based structure of regime complexes across five major issue areas: development finance, financial stability, tax avoidance, intellectual property rights, and energy governance. Drawing on original quantitative and qualitative data, it introduces a new theoretical framework for understanding how clusters of overlapping international institutions are organised and how they shift under geopolitical pressure.

What I Work On

Research Areas

🏛️

International Institutions & Regime Complexity

How do overlapping international institutions interact, compete, and evolve? I develop frameworks to map and compare the structure of institutional ecosystems — regime complexes — across global governance domains, from finance to climate to digital trade.

⚡

Geoeconomic Security & De-Risking

How do states respond when economic interdependence becomes a security liability? I study how Europe and other democracies design de-risking strategies to reduce vulnerabilities in AI, semiconductors, critical raw materials, digital infrastructure, and energy supply chains.

🌐

Autocratic Influence & Digital Governance

How do authoritarian-aligned actors use the openness of multilateral institutions and technical standard-setting bodies — such as ICANN, the ITU, and 3GPP — to embed illiberal rules? I examine the governance of AI, internet infrastructure, and digital standards as sites of geopolitical contestation.
